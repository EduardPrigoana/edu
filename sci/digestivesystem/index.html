The Digestive System

Mouth

What Happens Here

Digestion begins in the mouth. Food is mechanically broken down by the teeth through chewing (mastication). The tongue helps mix food with saliva to form a soft mass called a bolus, which is then swallowed.

Key Processes

  • Mechanical digestion: Teeth grind and tear food into smaller pieces
  • Chemical digestion: Saliva begins breaking down starches
  • Bolus formation: Food is mixed with saliva for easier swallowing

Enzymes Released

  • Salivary Amylase (Ptyalin): Breaks down starch into maltose
  • Lingual Lipase: Begins the digestion of fats (minor role)

Oesophagus

What Happens Here

The oesophagus is a muscular tube approximately 25cm long that connects the mouth to the stomach. Food does not simply fall down; it is actively pushed through by rhythmic muscle contractions called peristalsis.

Key Processes

  • Peristalsis: Wave-like muscle contractions push food toward the stomach
  • The epiglottis closes over the windpipe to prevent food entering the lungs
  • The lower oesophageal sphincter opens to allow food into the stomach

Enzymes Released

  • None: The oesophagus does not produce digestive enzymes
  • It secretes mucus to lubricate food passage

Stomach

What Happens Here

The stomach is a J-shaped muscular organ that churns food and mixes it with gastric juices. Food is broken down into a thick, acidic liquid called chyme. The stomach can hold approximately 1 to 1.5 litres of food.

Key Processes

  • Churning: Strong muscular contractions mix food with gastric juices
  • Protein digestion begins with the enzyme pepsin
  • Hydrochloric acid kills bacteria and creates an acidic environment (pH 1.5-3.5)
  • Food remains in the stomach for 2-6 hours

Enzymes Released

  • Pepsin: Breaks proteins into smaller peptides
  • Gastric Lipase: Continues the digestion of fats
  • Hydrochloric Acid: Activates pepsin and kills microorganisms

Small Intestine

What Happens Here

Despite its name, the small intestine is approximately 6 metres long. This is where most chemical digestion and nutrient absorption occurs. The walls are lined with tiny finger-like projections called villi that increase surface area for absorption.

Key Processes

  • Duodenum: Receives chyme from stomach, bile from liver, and enzymes from pancreas
  • Jejunum: Primary site of nutrient absorption
  • Ileum: Absorbs vitamin B12, bile salts, and remaining nutrients

Enzymes Released

  • Maltase, Sucrase, Lactase: Break down sugars into glucose
  • Peptidases: Complete protein digestion into amino acids
  • Intestinal Lipase: Breaks down fats into fatty acids

Accessory Organs

Liver

The largest internal organ. It produces bile (500-1000ml daily) which emulsifies fats, breaking them into smaller droplets for easier digestion. The liver also processes absorbed nutrients and detoxifies harmful substances.

Gallbladder

A small pear-shaped organ that stores and concentrates bile. When fatty food enters the duodenum, the gallbladder contracts and releases bile through the bile duct.

Pancreas

Produces pancreatic juice containing powerful digestive enzymes and bicarbonate to neutralise stomach acid in the duodenum.

Pancreatic Enzymes

  • Pancreatic Amylase: Digests carbohydrates into sugars
  • Trypsin and Chymotrypsin: Digest proteins into peptides
  • Pancreatic Lipase: Primary enzyme for fat digestion

Large Intestine

What Happens Here

The large intestine (colon) is approximately 1.5 metres long and 6cm wide. Its primary function is to absorb water and electrolytes from indigestible food matter and form solid faeces.

Key Processes

  • Water absorption: Up to 1.5 litres of water is absorbed daily
  • Bacterial fermentation: Gut bacteria break down remaining material
  • Vitamin production: Bacteria synthesise vitamins K and B
  • Compaction: Waste material is formed into faeces

Enzymes Released

  • None: The large intestine does not produce digestive enzymes
  • Gut bacteria produce enzymes that ferment fibre

Rectum

What Happens Here

The rectum is a chamber approximately 12-15cm long that connects the large intestine to the anus. It serves as a temporary storage area for faeces before elimination from the body.

Key Processes

  • Storage: Holds faeces until it is convenient to defecate
  • Stretch receptors: Nerve endings detect when the rectum is full
  • Defecation reflex: Signals are sent to the brain creating the urge to defecate
  • The walls can expand to accommodate increasing amounts of material

Enzymes Released

  • None: The rectum does not produce digestive enzymes
  • Mucus is secreted to lubricate the passage of faeces

Anus

What Happens Here

The anus is the final part of the digestive tract. It is the opening through which faeces are expelled from the body during defecation. The anus is controlled by two sphincter muscles.

Key Structures

  • Internal anal sphincter: Involuntary muscle that relaxes automatically
  • External anal sphincter: Voluntary muscle under conscious control
  • Defecation occurs when both sphincters relax in coordination
  • The process marks the end of the digestive journey

Enzymes Released

  • None: The anus does not produce any digestive enzymes
  • No chemical digestion occurs at this stage
